HL7 Conference 2012

Report on conference

Sunday

FHIR Hands-on

Grahame Greaves presentation on how to integrate with development of FHIR. FHIR is pronounced "FIRE". Not a class on how to implement. FHIR is new and builds upon both HL7 v2 and HL7 v3. My take on this is that FHIR is the next generation after v3. HL7 v3 was built to support modelers but was not good for hands one. FHIR is focused on hands on, getting connections accomplished, fast. Highly focused on IMPLEMENTATION. This is a standard that might one day replace our HL7 v2 messages. Here is an opportunity for us to become involved on a domain committee to represent immunizations.

Attended orientation meeting and listened to an introduction to the work group and standards development process.

Monday

Open Plenary

Leslie Kelly Hall from Health Wise
Talking about patient generated data. Data that is created by and sent by patient's themselves. Spoke about the need to "Liberate Patient Data". Listed the following progressive steps: (1) Inform Me (2) Engage Me (3) Empower Me (4) Partner with Me.

Lee Rainie from Pew Internet Project
Pew Internet Project is a Fact Tank. Talking about new network age in healthcare. Neworked Individulism. Social networks are more important than they used to be. (I'm not so sure that they are so different, rather that the networks are now more online than they have ever been.) Talking about technology usage by Americans, Internet usage is high, now more smart phones than regular cell phones, already 30% of those have at least one health app on their phone. Social networking use is increasing, even among 65+. Half of searches for health questions are done on behalf of others. Things are changing a lot, be not afraid! Questions about how people might share a lot of information online.

Panel Discussion

Chuck Parker from Continua Health Alliance
Focused on making guidelines for implementing certification program for sensors that can be used to track patients and help them manage their disease with the goal of keeping the patient from going to acute care. HL7 standards have been critical to smoothing adoption and integrating these systems.

Chris Lehmann from Vanderbilt
AAP has received a grant to work on developing a model EHR format. They developed a description of an ideal pediatric EHR based on HL7 standards and selected home-grown EHR's that demonstrate best practice. Meetings included 11 vendors who came to AAP and asked for more guidance on algorithms. Group eventually created web service to communicate and implement Bright Futures.

Jim St. Clair from HIMSS
Talking about mHiMMS process. Talked about mobile hardware penetration and the future use of health applications. Right data, right time, right place.

Lonnie Smith from FDA
Talked about Structured Product Labels (SPL). These are statements that are made by manufacturers and send to the FDA, which sends them to the National Library of Medicine (NLM) which operates a web site for doctors and consumers to look up drug medication. The site is called Daily Med.

Heather Grain from Standards Australia
Talked about the view from consumers. Very interesting. Talked about the great variety of opinions that consumers have and the need for health care professionals to listen them. Particularly important is that consumers want control over their information and how it's shared. Compared changes in the last 10 years:

  • Afraid of tech --> Already happened
  • No idea of benefit --> Long list of benefits
  • Strong idea of risks --> Understand risks, feel need to balance

Spoke about how surprising it is to find that some things that you would think are NOT sensitive are considered highly sensitive to some consumers, while highly sensitive issues are considered critical to share by other consumers.

Charles Jaffe
Talked a little about board's decision to make large parts of IP open source. Meeting during lunch to talk about implication for HL7 as an organization.

Lunch Meeting about Future of HL7

CEO Charles Jaffe lead a discussion to a fairly full room of HL7 members. He explained that August 1st the HL7 board voted to make HL7 IP freely available, whereas currently it is available only for HL7 members or for purchase. Charles Jaffe asked what directly should HL7 take in order to continue growing the organization. He called first on a member from Kaiser Permamente who indicated that a change that is needed is for membership to be offered on a multi-year basis, with yearly payments, in order to help HL7 have more steady funding. He also said that Kaiser Permamente indicated a commitment to purchase a multi-year organizational membership. Charles Jaffe went on to say that since the announcement and the idea from Kaiser Peramente they have nearly 20 benefactors who have come forward with similar commitments.

Discussion then turned to several questions:

  • Should HL7 offer tooling software? This is something the board has been asking HL7 to do. Quite a big challange, HL7 is not a software organization per se.
  • Should HL7 offer consulting services? Currently requests for consulting assistance is referred to HL7 members known to provide this.
  • Should HL7 become more like a professional organization with service to HL7 professionals?
  • Should HL7 rely more on the conferences for income? Currently conferences are break-even, but perhaps significant income could be generated by opening conference to vendors.
  • Should HL7 charge higher memberships for government entities? Currently organizations, such as CMS, benefit directly and substantially by the work of HL7, but each entity only pays the organizational membership fee of $100. Some comments were made from states and other governments about current restrictions that make even paying for attendance at HL7 meetings a large barrier and that more funding is not likely.

The underlying fear is that current interest for membership is driven by the need of organizations to receive the specification, if it is free then they will no longer pay for membership. HL7 is currently designed and built by volunteers who are willing to pay membership fees. Some comments were made that the interest for HL7 will increase tremendously as this goes public and that while we may loose some members we will gain many more interested in working on an open project.

My Thoughts

The changes to the HL7 organization are exciting and offer an opportunity for the HL7 community at large to give input about how HL7 organization could better help the entire community. I have a few ideas in this area. But before I give my ideas it's important to establish a few things first:

Background: HL7 users comes from one of two basic backgrounds: medical & technical. Medical professionals are attracted to HL7 as they work to pull systems together, technical professionals as they try to put these medical systems together. HL7 has always had a very technical, geeky, background, which I think is its strength. It needs to grow by helping the medical community understand the standards better and to help the technical community understand the medical aspects.

Level of Involvement: There are three basic levels of involvement in HL7. The original and most visible is that of the HL7 standards development. This level is where the current active members of HL7 operate at. HL7 spends a considerable amount of effort in this area. The second level is that of active implementors, and the third is active operators of HL7 interfaces. The size of the operators group is much larger than the implementors group, which is itself much larger than the development group.

Here is what HL7 could do to support each of these three groups:

  • HL7 Standard Operator: Offer online training and certification of operator level abilities. This training should not take too long or cost too much, and should offer two areas of focus, medical and technical. The idea behind the training would be to familiarize the student on the HL7 standard and how it can be used to help interoperability. It would also be good to require continuing education, at least every 2 or 3 years, to continue to hold certification.
  • HL7 Standard Implementor: Expand the current education and training so that it includes both an in-person component and a online process. The online process should be built to keep implementors up-to-date on latest changes and updates and allow for keeping certification current. Certification at this level could be renewed every year or two. Perhaps the initial certification expense is good, but ongoing certification should not be too expensive.
  • HL7 Standard Developer: A developer, who is active in HL7 and contributes, should receive the highest level of certification. This person should be actively participating in HL7, mentoring implementers and operators, promoting HL7, training HL7 users, and helping to develop standards. Active developers should have reduced costs to be active members as they are producing a great value to the community.

User Group Meetings: HL7 currently divides development work into a set of volunteer workgroups and is always looking for new members to staff these organizations. There is a large untapped resource of Implementors and Operators who currently do not participate in these groups. The reasons for this probably range from (1) they don't know about the workgroups and (2) they don't have time to contribute to the process. HL7 organization is missing a large potential by not engaging this group directly. What would be very useful to this group would be to form a set of User Groups built around specific tops that are placed underneath the work groups, and are mentored by the work groups. These user groups could simply be regular conference calls that allow users to talk about implementation problems they are encountering and get help from the larger group. This type of setup would likely have a large amount of interest and would introduce people to HL7 organization and some of these would likely want to eventually help with development.
